Unit 3.4

Long-Run Aggregate Supply

What happens when ALL prices in the economy, including wages and contracts, have time to fully adjust.

📊

Why Is LRAS Vertical?

In the long run, input prices are fully flexible. If the price level rises, wages and material costs rise too. Real profits don't change, so firms have no reason to produce more or less.

LRAS is vertical at Yf (full-employment output)
🔼
PL Goes Up?
Costs go up too. Real profits unchanged. Same output.
🔽
PL Goes Down?
Costs go down too. Real profits unchanged. Same output.
Think of it like this: Your school has a fixed number of classrooms, teachers, and hours in the day. Raising or lowering tuition doesn't change how many students you can teach. That maximum capacity = Yf.
LRAS = The PPC of the Whole Economy

Both represent the economy's maximum sustainable output using all resources efficiently. Whatever shifts the PPC outward also shifts LRAS right.

🔄

What Shifts LRAS?

LRAS shifts when the economy's productive capacity changes. Not costs. Capacity.

📈 LRAS Shifts RIGHT
More/better resources: Immigration, new oil fields discovered
Technology: AI revolution, automation breakthroughs
Human capital: More college grads, better training
Institutions: Property rights, open trade, rule of law
📉 LRAS Shifts LEFT
Resource destruction: War, natural disasters
Brain drain: Skilled workers emigrating
Institutional collapse: Corruption, instability
Resource depletion: Running out of key resources
Key Difference: SRAS shifts from changes in costs (wages, oil prices, taxes). LRAS shifts from changes in capacity (resources, technology, human capital). Some things, like technology, shift BOTH.

Key Concept

Output Gaps Explained

When the short-run equilibrium doesn't line up with full employment, we have an output gap.

📉 Recessionary Gap

GDP < Yf

High unemployment (above natural rate)
Low/falling prices
Businesses have excess capacity

Real life: COVID lockdowns in 2020. Massive unemployment, businesses shut down.

⚖️ Long-Run Equilibrium

GDP = Yf

At natural rate of unemployment
Stable prices
All three curves intersect

Real life: Mid-2019 economy. Low unemployment, moderate inflation, steady growth.

📈 Inflationary Gap

GDP > Yf

Very low unemployment (below natural rate)
Rising prices
Economy is "overheating"

Real life: Post-COVID 2021-22. Stimulus spending, 9% inflation, "Now Hiring" everywhere.
⚠️

Stagflation: The Worst of Both Worlds

When SRAS shifts LEFT, you get rising prices AND falling output at the same time. This is called stagflation (stagnation + inflation).

SRAS ← Left → PL ↑ + GDP ↓ = Stagflation
1970s Oil Crisis: OPEC cut oil supply, gas prices skyrocketed, and the U.S. had both high unemployment AND high inflation simultaneously. It's the scenario policymakers fear most because fixing one problem makes the other worse.
